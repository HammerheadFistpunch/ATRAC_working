Here's a summary of every change made and why:
attackFactor — accumulator overpressure (both files)
Default lifted from 0.85 to 1.80, jbeam max raised from 1.00 to 3.00. The multiplier can now exceed wd.brakeTorque to model the accumulator's hydraulic pressure exceeding what the pedal circuit can generate. The perWheelTorqueCap backstop is untouched — engine stall protection still applies.
Attack → Hold gate (lua)
Previously the code transitioned out of attack on a single frame where slip < threshold. Now it requires both: a slipClearDwell timer (default 50ms — rules out momentary dips) AND avgDrivenWheelSpeed >= forwardProgressThreshold (default 0.3 m/s — confirms the vehicle is actually climbing, not just un-spinning). Until both pass, the attack pressure holds with solenoids sealed.
Airborne pressure freeze (lua)
Removed the state.phase = "idle" reset on airborne. Instead, if the wheel was already intervening, it writes frozenTorque — the last pressure value — to desiredBrakingTorque. Phase and timers are preserved. On re-contact the wheel resumes exactly where it was. Idle wheels while airborne still leave brakeControl alone.
frozenTorque tracking
Every time the state machine writes desiredBrakingTorque it also saves the value to state.frozenTorque. This is what the airborne freeze reads, and it decays naturally to zero when the wheel finally reaches idle.
New jbeam variables
$atrac_slipClearDwell (ms) and $atrac_fwdProgressThresh (m/s) wired through to the atracTorqueCap data block, init(), and setParameters().

slipClearDwell largely duplicates releaseHoldTime
Both are "hold pressure after slip clears" timers. slipClearDwell does it at full attack pressure; releaseHoldTime does it at releaseHoldFactor. They stack additively — the system holds for 50ms at full attack, then another 120ms at reduced pressure. That's 170ms of total "don't let go" time spread across two separate knobs. You could achieve the same tuning range with just releaseHoldTime alone (simply make it longer and/or keep releaseHoldFactor higher at the start of release1). slipClearDwell is the most expendable parameter added.
The AND-gate between slipClearDwell and forwardProgressThreshold is the likely source of "working less well"
In slow rock crawling, both conditions must be simultaneously true: slip clear for 50ms and wheel surface speed ≥ 0.3 m/s. The problem is that in the attack phase, any single frame where slip spikes back above threshold resets slipClearTimer to zero — so the 50ms clock restarts constantly. On highly uneven terrain the timer may never complete, locking the system in full attack indefinitely. forwardProgressThreshold alone (without the dwell) might be sufficient, and it's the more physically meaningful of the two conditions.
interventionHoldTime has partial overlap with the dwell/hold chain
Its actual job is to freeze torque cap decay. But since safetyMarginCoef = 1.4 keeps the cap permissive anyway, it only matters if the bang-bang command itself has already released and the cap has drifted down. If the bang-bang release phases are tuned correctly, the cap hold is mostly redundant insurance. Not urgent to remove, but it's a fourth timer (alongside slipClearDwell, releaseHoldTime, releaseBleedTime) governing the same post-slip window.
The parameters that are genuinely non-redundant and doing distinct jobs:
slipThreshold, attackFactor, releaseHoldFactor, releaseBleedTime, forwardProgressThreshold, torqueFloor, safetyMarginCoef, and torqueDecayRate all control distinct, non-overlapping aspects of the system. The brakeControl params (kP, kI, slipRangeThreshold, maxVelocity, velocityOffsetThresh) are upstream and separate.
Bottom line: slipClearDwell is the parameter most worth removing — collapse it into a slightly longer releaseHoldTime.
